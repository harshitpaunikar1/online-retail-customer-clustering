Who Actually Shops Here:
Five Customer Archetypes

Transaction data doesn't contain customers — it contains receipts. The work of segmentation is converting 541,000 line items into recognizable human patterns that a merchandising team can act on.

The Problem with Raw Transaction Data

The UCI Online Retail dataset records every purchase from a UK gift-goods wholesaler between December 2010 and December 2011. It has 541,909 rows. Each row is a stock item on an invoice — quantity, unit price, customer ID, country, date. In that form it is a ledger, not an understanding of customers.

The first transformation that makes segmentation possible is collapsing those rows to one row per customer with aggregate behavioral features. A customer who placed 12 orders totaling £4,200 over 11 months, with their most recent order 8 days before the data cutoff, is a fundamentally different entity from a customer who placed one order for £85 fourteen months ago. The ledger can't tell you that; the per-customer summary can.

The standard aggregation framework for this purpose is RFM: Recency, Frequency, Monetary. These three dimensions capture most of the behavioral variation that drives customer value and informs retention, acquisition, and win-back strategy. They are also interpretable without statistical training, which matters when the segmentation outputs need to land with a campaign manager who did not build the model.

Recency is the number of days since the customer's last purchase, measured from the data cutoff date. Low recency (purchased recently) is associated with higher probability of repeat purchase. High recency signals dormancy or departure.

Frequency is the number of distinct invoices (orders) the customer placed. Frequency separates one-time buyers from occasional shoppers from habitual purchasers. For a gift-goods retailer with seasonal demand, frequency also captures whether a customer has transcended the "occasional occasion" mindset and is now a regular reorder account — likely a wholesaler.

Monetary is the total revenue the customer generated. Raw monetary value is correlated with frequency (more orders = more spending), so it is useful in combination with frequency to identify high-average-order customers who buy infrequently but spend heavily per visit.

Building the Feature Table

import pandas as pd
from datetime import datetime

df = pd.read_csv('Online_Retail.csv', encoding='latin1')

# Clean: drop missing customer IDs, remove returns (negative quantity)
df = df.dropna(subset=['CustomerID'])
df = df[df['Quantity'] > 0]
df['Revenue'] = df['Quantity'] * df['UnitPrice']

# Snapshot date: day after last invoice in dataset
snapshot = df['InvoiceDate'].max() + pd.Timedelta(days=1)

rfm = df.groupby('CustomerID').agg(
    Recency   = ('InvoiceDate', lambda x: (snapshot - x.max()).days),
    Frequency = ('InvoiceNo',   'nunique'),   # distinct orders
    Monetary  = ('Revenue',     'sum')
).reset_index()

# Log-transform Monetary: right-skewed, dominated by wholesaler spend
import numpy as np
rfm['Monetary_log'] = np.log1p(rfm['Monetary'])

# StandardScaler before K-means: R, F, M are different units
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
scaler = StandardScaler()
rfm_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(rfm[['Recency','Frequency','Monetary_log']])

One data quality decision matters here: the dataset contains negative quantities, which are returns. Including them in revenue totals would understate monetary value for customers who make frequent returns. The notebook filters these out, treating net revenue as the relevant measure. Whether that is the right choice depends on whether return behavior itself is a signal worth preserving as a separate feature — for this analysis it is not, but a production segmentation system might add a return_rate feature to the RFM base.

The UK-based versus international customer split is another decision point. International customers (Germany, France, Australia) have different purchasing patterns partly because of shipping economics and partly because they index more toward wholesale. Including them in the same clustering pool produces mixed clusters that blend domestic retail behavior with international wholesale behavior. The notebook's choice to include all customers is defensible for a first-pass segmentation but would warrant country stratification in a production system.

The Centroid Gallery: Five Archetypes

With a Silhouette score of 0.54 at k=4 and 0.51 at k=5, the data supports 4–5 clusters reasonably well. The k=5 solution produces five interpretable behavioral archetypes that map onto recognizable customer types for a gift-goods retailer.

Cluster Separation and What the Geometry Says

From Archetypes to Merchandising Decisions

The cluster labels are not the output — they are the input to campaign and merchandising decisions. Each archetype implies a different relationship with product mix, promotional price, and message timing.

Champions: Protect, Don't Discount

Champions are already buying frequently and spending at the top of the range. The mistake retailers make with this group is sending them the same promotional discount that goes to everyone else — which trains them to wait for promotions, reducing full-price purchase rate. The right strategy is exclusivity, not price reduction: early access to new product lines, personalised wholesale pricing for the business buyers in this cluster, and acknowledgment of tenure. This group should never receive a generic "15% off everything" email blast.

Loyal Regulars: Cross-Category Expansion

Loyal Regulars have demonstrated consistent purchasing in their established category. The opportunity is category expansion: they bought Christmas decorations — do they also need birthday gifts? They bought tabletop items — do they also buy occasion stationery? Recommendation engines built on frequently-bought-together patterns within this cluster can suggest the adjacent category that their purchase history suggests but doesn't yet include. The pitch is convenience ("you might also like") rather than discount.

Promising Newcomers: Second Purchase Is the Conversion Event

For a gift-goods retailer, the most important behavioral transition is from one-time buyer to habitual shopper. A customer who places a second order within 30 days of their first has fundamentally changed their relationship with the retailer — they have discovered a catalog and an experience they trust. The acquisition funnel's job is to make the first order happen; the CRM system's job is to manufacture a reason for the second order within the window when the first purchase memory is still fresh. Targeted post-purchase email within 72 hours, tied to a specific complementary product recommendation, converts more of this group than a generic "thanks for your order" message.

At-Risk Dormants: Time-Boxed Win-Back

Win-back campaigns have a narrow effective window. Research across retail categories suggests that customers who have been silent for more than 180 days have much lower win-back rates than customers at 90–150 days. The intervention is time-sensitive: a strong incentive (meaningful discount, free gift with order) at the 90-day silence mark is more cost-effective than the same incentive at 270 days. At-Risk Dormants should be flagged as soon as they cross a silence threshold, not at the next quarterly campaign batch.

One-Time Occasions: Low Cost, Seasonal Focus

This is the largest cluster and the most expensive to over-invest in. The customers in this group bought for a specific moment — a birthday, a holiday, a wedding gift — and have not found a reason to return. Forcing high-frequency communication at this group produces unsubscribes without incremental revenue. The right approach is seasonal alignment: contact them once before Christmas, once before Valentine's Day, once before Mother's Day. If none of those seasonal triggers produces a second order after 12 months, the cost of continued outreach exceeds the expected return.

The Silhouette Score and What It Actually Means

The silhouette scores reported in the notebook (0.54 at k=4, 0.51 at k=5) indicate moderate cluster separation — better than random assignment, short of tight separation. A score of 0.54 does not mean the clusters are wrong; it means the boundaries between adjacent clusters are fuzzy, which is accurate — real customer behavior does not have hard edges. The valuable insight is not that every customer falls cleanly into one category, but that the centroid of each cluster represents a recognizable type with actionable implications.

The score also depends on the feature set. RFM computed on 12 months of data captures behavioral history but not forward-looking signals like time-to-event churn risk or category diversity score. Adding a fourth dimension — category_breadth (number of distinct product categories a customer has purchased) — would sharpen the Champions vs. Loyal Regulars boundary, because Champions in this dataset tend to buy across many gift categories while Loyal Regulars concentrate in two or three. A five-dimensional RFM-plus-breadth model is a natural next step for a production segmentation system on this dataset.
